# The Only Way Retail Money Survives the AI Bull Market

This is not investment advice.

It is only my personal view and a record of how I am thinking about the AI market cycle.

Over the past two years, the market has been playing a strange game:

If you cannot buy the real protagonist, buy the protagonist’s shadow.

If you cannot buy OpenAI or Anthropic, buy Microsoft, Google, and Amazon.

If you cannot buy SpaceX, buy Rocket Lab.

If you cannot buy AGI, buy every company with “AI” somewhere in the story.

This does not mean the market is stupid.

It means the market has no choice.

Because many of the true core assets are still not available in public markets.

So a large amount of capital has been forced to search for substitutes.

And many assets that have risen over the past few years have not only benefited from fundamentals. They have also benefited from the substitute premium created by the absence of the real crown jewels.

But this logic may have one fatal problem:

> Once the real protagonist appears, the shadows get repriced.

That, to me, may be one of the most important market questions of the next few years.

## The AI bull market has been a liquidity spillover

In my view, the AI bull market of the past two years has partly been a liquidity spillover caused by the lack of true AI crown-jewel assets in public markets.

The strongest assets were not available to ordinary investors.

You could not buy OpenAI.

You could not buy Anthropic.

You could not buy SpaceX.

So capital had to find another route.

It bought Nvidia.

It bought Microsoft.

It bought cloud companies.

It bought second-tier AI names.

It bought AI concept stocks.

It bought almost anything that looked even remotely connected.

This created a strange market structure.

Many companies that are still very far away from AGI began to trade at aggressively expanded valuations.

Because what the market was really buying was not always the company itself.

It was buying anxiety about missing the AI main table.

This also explains why more and more AI stocks have started to feel a little like the year 2000.

Not because AI is fake.

Quite the opposite.

Because AI may be very real, but there are too few truly core assets available to buy.

So liquidity has crowded into anything that might benefit directly or indirectly.

But this situation may not last forever.

If OpenAI, Anthropic, or SpaceX eventually become accessible to public market investors, the entire liquidity structure could be reshuffled.

Many people are still asking:

“What is the next small AI stock?”

But the better question may be:

> If OpenAI and Anthropic can actually be bought, what are the substitutes still worth?

Money does not disappear.

It simply gets back in line.

At least if the macro liquidity backdrop remains relatively stable, I think capital will begin to fight for the real names.

And once that process begins, the market may become much more brutal.

---

## AI is likely a winner-take-most game

AI is an industry that naturally favors concentration.

It depends heavily on compute, data, model capability, talent density, capital expenditure, distribution, and ecosystem.

That means the leaders can keep getting stronger.

And the laggards may find it harder and harder to catch up.

In the end, a large portion of liquidity may concentrate in only a handful of super-core assets.

This is where many retail investors may be thinking about the game incorrectly.

A lot of people believe that with small capital, they should spread money across many small and medium-sized companies. Maybe one of them becomes the big winner. Maybe diversification protects them.

But I think the greatest advantage of small capital may actually be the ability to focus.

Private funds and large public funds cannot easily go all in on one direction.

Ordinary investors can.

That is the real weapon of small capital.

Because the kind of asset that changes wealth distribution is usually not a random side story.

It is the core asset of an era-level industrial revolution.

The internet era was like this.

The mobile internet era was like this.

The AI era may be the same.

The problem is that most retail investors do not really buy “the era.”

They buy emotional, hot, concept-driven, high-beta substitutes.

Those assets often feel faster.

More exciting.

More likely to double.

But the real era-level capital may eventually concentrate more and more.

## The lesson from the internet bubble

During the internet bubble around 2000, there were countless internet companies.

In the end, only a small number truly survived and became dominant.

Amazon.

Google.

Microsoft.

And a few others.

Most of the rest disappeared so thoroughly that people no longer remember their names.

The AI era may rhyme with that.

There may be thousands of AI-related companies.

But the final main table may only have a few seats.

So the most important question for the next few years may not be:

“Which AI small cap has the most upside?”

It may be:

> Who will become the final main-table player of the AI era?

To me, a main-table player needs at least three things.

First, it must sit near the top of the value chain.

It defines rules instead of being defined by them.

Second, it must own resources that others cannot easily copy in the short term.

Models.

Data.

Compute.

Distribution.

Talent.

Ecosystem.

It does not need to dominate all of them, but it probably needs to control at least two or three.

Third, once it becomes available in public markets, it should make many of the old substitutes feel uncomfortable.

In my view, OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX all have the characteristics of main-table players.

If companies like these become publicly investable, the market is not simply buying “a new ticker.”

It is buying the thing investors were previously forced to express indirectly.

That is why I think shadow assets may be repriced.

Not necessarily because the shadows are bad companies.

But because once the real asset appears, the market will ask a very simple question:

> Why should I keep using a second-tier substitute to express a first-tier belief?

## The retail investor’s real chance

For retail investors, I think the next few years may come down to four things.

First:

Stand on the main AI highway.

Second:

Get as close as possible to the true core assets, instead of forever chasing shadows on the edge.

Third:

Start preparing buying power before the era’s core assets become accessible.

Because the biggest opportunities are often not about whether you know they exist.

They are about whether you still have capital when the door opens.

Fourth:

Stay on the side of the long-term winners.

Not always on the edge of the market searching for the next miracle.

The temptation for small capital is always the same.

Find something obscure.

Find something explosive.

Find something that can 5x before anyone else notices.

Sometimes that works.

But in an era-level cycle, the biggest money may not come from being the cleverest person in the smallest room.

It may come from sitting closer to the main table.

Retail capital is small.

That is its weakness.

But small capital is also flexible.

That is its strength.

It can wait.

It can concentrate.

It can move before institutions are allowed to move.

It can prepare dry powder before the obvious asset becomes available.

The only question is whether the investor spends all their energy chasing shadows before the real door opens.

## Final thought

The AI bull market has created enormous excitement.

But it has also created a strange problem:

The market has been asked to price an era before many of the era’s most important assets are even available.

That is why so many shadows have been bid up.

That is also why the next phase may be more selective, more concentrated, and more brutal.

If the real crown jewels arrive in public markets, the market may not simply add more AI exposure on top of everything else.

It may reshuffle the existing exposure.

The shadows will not all disappear.

Some may still be excellent businesses.

Some may still compound.

Some may still deserve to exist.

But the premium attached to “being the closest available substitute” may no longer be the same.

For retail investors, the lesson is not to chase every AI ticker.

It is to ask a harder question:

Am I buying the era?

Or am I buying the shadow of the era because the real thing is not yet available?

In my view, the only way retail money survives the AI bull market is not by constantly hunting for the next miracle.

It is by staying close to the core.

Keeping buying power.

And being ready when the main table finally opens.
